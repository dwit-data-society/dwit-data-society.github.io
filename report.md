Report on Company Survey Conducted During the Job Fair
Introduction

As part of the college job fair, a survey was conducted among participating companies to better understand current hiring practices, workplace structures, and opportunities available to students entering the workforce. Responses were collected from 15 companies, most of which operate in the IT and software sector and primarily function at the national level within Nepal. Since participation was voluntary, the sample should be understood as a convenience sample rather than a representative cross-section of all companies in Nepal or even all companies present at the fair. Furthermore, all information was self-reported by company representatives and was not independently verified. The analysis presented here should therefore be interpreted as exploratory rather than conclusive. Its value lies less in making definitive claims and more in revealing patterns, assumptions, and variations across companies that may help students, faculty, and administrators better understand the current employment landscape.

Employees in Nepal vs. Abroad

The surveyed companies collectively reported approximately 6,113 employees, of whom around 91.5% were based in Nepal and 8.5% abroad. At first glance, this suggests that the participating companies maintain a strong domestic workforce presence despite operating in globally connected industries such as software and IT services. However, the aggregate numbers are heavily influenced by a few large firms with thousands of employees, meaning the overall percentages should not be interpreted as typical of every company in the sample. In addition, some companies reported entirely Nepal-based workforces while others maintained substantial international operations, indicating considerable variation in business models. These figures therefore reflect the composition of this particular sample more than the structure of the Nepali technology sector as a whole.

Entry-Level Positions Offered

Across the 15 companies, a total of 249 annual entry-level positions were reported. The median company reported around 8 entry-level openings per year, although the distribution was highly uneven. A few larger companies accounted for a disproportionately high number of openings, while smaller firms reported far fewer positions. Some small organizations appeared to offer a large number of entry-level opportunities relative to their size, though such figures may reflect internships, temporary roles, or rapidly expanding teams rather than stable long-term hiring capacity. This variability highlights an important feature of the job market for students: opportunities may depend not only on company size, but also on growth stage, business strategy, and turnover. Since all figures were self-reported and no standardized definition of “entry-level position” was enforced during data collection, comparisons between companies should be interpreted cautiously.

Companies Currently Hiring

All surveyed companies indicated at least some entry-level hiring activity, though only 7 of the 15 companies provided evidence of a recent hire date. In this analysis, “currently hiring” was inferred indirectly through a combination of reported entry-level openings and the presence of recent hiring records. This assumption is important because companies may advertise openings without actively recruiting at the time of the survey, while others may recruit informally without documenting recent hires. Consequently, the analysis should not be interpreted as a precise measure of current labor demand. Nevertheless, the results do suggest that the participating companies generally maintain ongoing recruitment pipelines, particularly for junior roles, which may indicate sustained demand for new graduates in the technology sector.

Qualities Valued in Hiring Interns and Employees

The ranking analysis suggests that companies tended to prioritize passion more highly than technical skills, soft skills, or prior experience when evaluating candidates for intern roles. On average, passion received the strongest ranking across firms, while experience was ranked lowest. This result is perhaps less surprising for entry-level or internship positions, where applicants often possess limited formal work experience and where eagerness and a willingness to learn may matter most. Even so, the meaning of each criterion likely varied across respondents: “passion” may have reflected motivation, curiosity, or cultural fit, while “experience” may have included project work, internships, portfolios, freelancing, or participation in technical communities rather than only full-time employment. Furthermore, rankings compress complex hiring decisions into simple numerical scales, potentially oversimplifying how companies actually evaluate applicants. The findings therefore reveal broad preferences rather than strict hiring rules. For students, the results may suggest that demonstrating genuine drive and a willingness to learn remains highly valued at the beginning stages of a career, even when formal experience is still limited.

Salary Growth with Years of Experience

The salary analysis indicates a substantial increase in estimated compensation as employees progress from entry-level to mid-level and senior-level positions. Median reported salaries rose from approximately NPR 25,000 at entry level to NPR 80,000 at mid-level and NPR 250,000 at senior level. However, interpreting these figures requires considerable caution. Some companies reported monthly salaries while others may have reported yearly estimates, reducing comparability across responses. Promotion timelines also varied significantly between firms, with some companies reporting advancement within less than a year and others taking several years for similar transitions. In addition, the analysis assumes a simplified progression from junior to senior roles, whereas real career paths are often nonlinear and influenced by specialization, company growth, and market demand. As a result, the observed salary growth should be interpreted not as a predictive career trajectory but as an approximate illustration of how compensation may evolve within certain segments of the Nepali IT industry.

Major Challenges Faced by Companies

The reported industry challenges were highly diverse, with relatively little overlap between companies. Concerns included competition, shortage of skilled manpower, political instability, changing technologies, workforce migration abroad, and increasing focus on artificial intelligence. Because most challenges were mentioned by only one company each, the results do not point to a single dominant issue affecting the entire sector. Instead, they reveal that different organizations experience the labor market and business environment differently depending on their size, specialization, and operational priorities. The repeated concern regarding excessive focus on AI is particularly interesting because it suggests tension between rapidly changing technological trends and practical workforce needs. At the same time, the small sample size and open-ended nature of the responses make it difficult to determine whether these concerns are widespread or simply more salient to the companies interviewed.

Gender Distribution within Companies

The surveyed companies collectively reported a workforce composition of approximately 60% male and 40% female employees. Ten companies fell within a broad “parity band” of 40–60% female representation, while several others reported significantly lower female participation. Although these findings may appear relatively balanced compared to stereotypical perceptions of the technology sector, they should be interpreted carefully. The figures were estimated and self-reported by company representatives rather than derived from audited employment records. Moreover, aggregate percentages do not capture important structural questions such as differences in seniority, leadership representation, salary equity, or technical versus non-technical roles. The analysis therefore provides only a limited view of gender representation. Still, it suggests that at least some companies are approaching more balanced workforce distributions than might traditionally be expected in IT-related fields.

Work Arrangement Preferences

Hybrid work arrangements were substantially more common than fully on-site work among the surveyed companies, with roughly 73% reporting hybrid models. This likely reflects broader workplace changes accelerated by remote collaboration technologies and evolving employee expectations after the COVID-19 pandemic. However, the meaning of “hybrid” may differ considerably between companies, ranging from occasional remote work to highly flexible arrangements. The analysis also does not distinguish between roles that require physical presence and those that can be performed remotely. Consequently, the results should not be interpreted as evidence that remote flexibility is universally available across the industry. Nevertheless, the prevalence of hybrid work among participating firms suggests that flexible work structures are becoming increasingly normalized within the Nepali technology sector.

Longest-Serving Employees, Benefits, and Satisfaction

The analysis explored possible relationships between employee tenure, entry-level salary, benefits, and reported satisfaction. The findings showed almost no correlation between tenure and entry-level salary, a moderate positive relationship between tenure and benefits offered, and a moderate negative relationship between tenure and reported satisfaction. However, these statistical relationships must be interpreted extremely carefully. The sample size is very small, and the satisfaction scores were estimated by HR or management representatives rather than directly measured from employees themselves. In addition, correlation does not imply causation. The negative relationship between tenure and satisfaction, for example, does not necessarily mean that long-serving employees are less satisfied. It may instead reflect organizational culture, company maturity, management expectations, or reporting bias. The results are therefore best understood as exploratory signals that raise further questions rather than providing definitive explanations about employee retention.

Training and Skill Development

Most surveyed companies reported offering skill development or training opportunities, with 11 out of 15 companies indicating the presence of training programs. Reported training frequencies varied considerably, although the median interval was approximately three years. Interpreting this result is somewhat difficult because the meaning of “training frequency” was not standardized during data collection. Some companies may have interpreted the question as formal certification cycles, while others may have considered routine workshops or mentorship sessions. Additionally, companies that did not offer formal training may still provide informal learning opportunities that were not captured by the survey. Despite these limitations, the findings suggest that many companies recognize the importance of continuous learning, particularly in rapidly evolving technical fields where employee skills can become outdated quickly.

Conclusion

Overall, the survey provides a useful but limited snapshot of hiring practices and workplace conditions among a small group of mostly IT-oriented companies participating in the college job fair. Several broad patterns emerged, including continued demand for entry-level workers, preference for practical experience, substantial salary growth with experience, increasing adoption of hybrid work arrangements, and widespread emphasis on training and skill development. At the same time, the analysis also reveals substantial diversity between companies, reminding us that no single organization can fully represent the wider employment market.

Perhaps the most important conclusion is methodological rather than statistical: the interpretation of data depends heavily on the assumptions used to collect, categorize, and analyze it. Because the sample was small, self-selected, and self-reported, the findings should not be generalized uncritically to the broader Nepali technology sector. Instead, the report should be viewed as a starting point for discussion and future investigation. Its value lies not in claiming certainty, but in helping students, faculty, and administrators think more carefully about the evolving relationship between education, employment, and the technology industry in Nepal.
