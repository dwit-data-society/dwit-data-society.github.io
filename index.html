Introduction

As part of the college job fair, a survey was conducted among participating companies to better understand current hiring practices, workplace structures, and opportunities available to students entering the workforce. Responses were collected from 15 companies, most of which operate in the IT and software sector and primarily function at the national level within Nepal.

Employees in Nepal vs. Abroad

The surveyed companies collectively reported approximately 6,113 employees, of whom around 91.5% were based in Nepal and 8.5 abroad. Some companies reported entirely Nepal-based workforces while others maintained some international operations.

Entry-level Positions Offered

Across the 15 companies, a total of 249 annual entry-level positions were reported. The median company reported around 8 entry-level openings per year. A few larger companies accounted for a disproportionately high number of openings. Since all figures were self-reported and no standardization definition of “entry-level” was enforced during data collection, comparisons between companies should be interpreted cautiously.

Salary Growth with Years of Experience

The salary analysis indicates a substantial increase in estimated compensation as employees progress from entry-level to mid-level and senior-level positions. Median reported salaries rose from approximately NPR 25,000 at entry level to NPR 80,000 at mid-level and NPR 2,50,000 at senior level. However, interpreting these figures requires considerable caution. Promotion timelines varied significantly between firms, with some companies reporting advancement within less than a year and other taking several years for similar transitions. The analysis assumes a simplified progression from junior to senior roles, whereas real career paths are often nonlinear and influence by specialization, company growth, and market demand. As a result, the observed salary growth should be interpreted not as a predictive career trajectory but as an approximate illustration of how compensation may evolve.

Major Challenges Faced by Companies

The reported industry challenges were highly diverse, with relatively little overlap between companies. Concerns included competition, shortage of skilled manpower, political instability, changing technologies, workforce migration abroad, and increasing focus on artificial intelligence. The repeated concern regarding excessive focus on Ai is interesting. It suggests there is tension between rapidly changing technological trends and practical workforce needs. At the same time, the small and open-ended nature of the responses make it difficult to determine whether these concerns are widespread.

Gender Distribution within Companies

The surveyed companies collectively reported a workforce composition of approximately 60% male and 40% female. Although these findings may appear relatively balanced compared to stereotypical perceptions of the technology sector, they should be interpreted carefully. The figures were estimated and self-reported by company representatives rather than derived from audited employment records. Aggregate percentages do not capture important structural questions such as differences in seniority, leadership representation, salary equity, or technical versus non-technical roles. The analysis provides only a limited view of gender representation. Still, it suggests that at least some companies are approaching more balanced workforce distributions.

Work Arrangement Preferences

Hybrid work arrangements were substantially more common than fully on-site work among the surveyed companies, with roughly 73% reporting hybrid models. However, the meaning of "hybrid" may differ considerably between companies, ranging from occasional remote work to highly flexible arrangements. The results should not be interpreted as evidence that remote work is universally available across the industry.

Longest-Serving Employees, Benefits, and Satisfaction

The analysis explored possible relationships between employee tenure, entry-level salary, benefits, and reported satisfaction. The findings showed almost no correlation between tenure and entry-level salary, a moderate positive relationship between tenure and benefits offered, and a moderate negative relationship between tenure and reported satisfaction. However, these statistical relationships must be interpreted extremely carefully. The sample size is very small, and the satisfaction scores were estimated by HR or management representatives rather than directly measured from employees themselves. In addition, correlation does not imply causation. The negative relationship between tenure and satisfaction, for example, does not necessarily mean that long-serving employees are less satisfied. It may instead reflect organizational culture, company maturity, management expectations, or reporting bias. The results are therefore best understood as exploratory signals that raise further questions rather than providing definitive explanations about employee retention.

Training and Skill Development

Most surveyed companies reported offering skill development or training opportunities, with 11 out of 15 companies indicating the presence of training programs. Reported training frequencies varied considerably, although the median interval was approximately three years. Interpreting this result is somewhat difficult because the meaning of "training frequency" was not standardized during data collection. Some companies may have interpreted the question as format certificate cycles, while others may have considered routine workshops or mentorship sessions. The findings suggest that many companies recognize the importance of continuous learning, particularly in rapidly evolving technical fields where employee skills can become outdated quickly.

Conclusion

The survey provides a useful but limited overview of hiring practices and workplace conditions among a small group of IT-oriented companies participating in the DWIT Job Fair. Several broad patterns emerged, including demand for entry-level workers, substantial salary growth with experience, increasing adoption of hybrid work arrangements, and emphasis on training and skill development.

The interpretation of data depends heavily on the assumptions used to collect, categorize, and analyze it. Because the sample was small, self-selected, and self-reported, the findings should not be generalized uncritically to the broader Nepali technology sector. The report should be viewed as a starting point for discussion.
